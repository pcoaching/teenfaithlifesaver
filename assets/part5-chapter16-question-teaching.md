# Chapter 16: When They Question Church Teaching
## The Crisis Protocol — Part 5

---

### The Difference Between Honest Questions and Rebellion

Not every question about Church teaching is a crisis. In fact, questions are often signs of a maturing faith. The teen who never questions is either unusually saintly or hasn't thought deeply about their faith.

**The key distinction:**

**Honest questioning:** Open, curious, willing to learn, seeking understanding

**Rebellion:** Closed, hostile, unwilling to listen, seeking justification for rejection

**Signs of honest questioning:**
- "Help me understand why the Church teaches..."
- "I've been reading about X, and I'm confused..."
- "I see the logic, but I struggle with the application..."
- "I want to believe this, but I have questions..."

**Signs of rebellion:**
- "The Church is wrong about this."
- "I don't care what the Church says."
- "That teaching is hateful/stupid/irrelevant."
- "I'm going to do what I want regardless."

**The same question can be honest or rebellious depending on attitude.** "Why does the Church oppose same-sex marriage?" can be a genuine search for understanding or a prelude to rejection. The words are the same — the heart is different.

---

## How to Discern the Difference

### Questions to Ask Yourself

**Is there openness to understanding?**

Honest questioners want to understand, even if they ultimately disagree. They'll read sources you suggest, talk to knowledgeable people, engage with the reasoning.

Rebellious questioners have already decided. They're looking for ammunition, not understanding. They dismiss sources before engaging them.

**Is the tone curious or hostile?**

Honest questioning sounds like: "I don't get this. Help me understand."

Rebellion sounds like: "This is ridiculous. How can anyone believe this?"

**Is there willingness to engage with resources?**

If you suggest a book, article, or conversation with a priest, do they accept? Or do they dismiss it immediately?

**Is the question isolated or part of wholesale rejection?**

One question about one teaching is normal. Questions about every teaching, combined with rejection of practice, is a larger crisis.

**Is there moral compromise involved?**

Often, questioning of sexual morality coincides with desire to engage in prohibited behavior. This doesn't invalidate the question — but it contextualizes it.

## Responding to Honest Questions

### The Dialogue Approach

**Step 1: Affirm the question.**

"That's a really important question. I'm glad you're thinking about this seriously."

**Step 2: Clarify what they actually want to know.**

"When you ask about [teaching], what specifically is confusing you? The reasoning, the application, or something else?"

**Step 3: Explain the Church's reasoning (if you know it).**

Don't just quote the Catechism. Explain the "why" behind the "what."

**Example — Same-sex marriage:**

"The Church teaches that marriage is the lifelong union of one man and one woman open to children. This isn't arbitrary. The Church believes:

1. Sexual union has a purpose — it's not just for pleasure but for bonding and procreation
2. Children have a right to be raised by their mother and father
3. The complementarity of male and female reflects God's design

The Church isn't saying same-sex attracted people are bad. It's saying marriage has a specific nature, and same-sex unions don't fit that nature. People with same-sex attraction are called to chastity, just as unmarried heterosexual people are."

**Step 4: Acknowledge difficulties.**

"I know this is hard. I know people you love may be in same-sex relationships. I know this teaching is unpopular. The Church asks something difficult here."

**Step 5: Offer resources for deeper understanding.**

"I don't have all the answers. Let's read something together, or talk to Father [name], or look into this more. I want to understand better myself."

**Step 6: Accept that they may still disagree.**

"I hope you'll come to accept this teaching, but I know you may not. Even if you disagree, I hope you'll understand why the Church teaches what it does. And I hope you'll keep asking questions and seeking truth."

## Common Questions and How to Address Them

### Sexual Morality Questions

**"Why does the Church care what I do in my private life?"**

**The reasoning:** The Church doesn't see sexuality as purely private. Sexual acts have consequences — emotional, physical, spiritual, social. The Church's teaching isn't about controlling people but about guiding them toward human flourishing.

**How to respond:** "The Church believes sexuality is powerful and sacred. It's not just a private preference like favorite ice cream. Sex has consequences — emotional attachment, potential for children, impact on your soul. The Church offers guidance not to restrict you but to help you flourish."

**"Why is contraception wrong?"**

**The reasoning:** Contraception separates the unitive and procreative aspects of sex. It treats fertility as a disease to be suppressed rather than a gift. It can lead to a contraceptive mentality where children are seen as burdens rather than blessings.

**How to respond:** "The Church sees sex as having two purposes — bonding between spouses and openness to children. Contraception deliberately blocks one of those purposes. It's not about having unlimited children — Natural Family Planning allows spacing children while remaining open to life. The question is whether we see fertility as a gift or a problem."

**"Why does the Church hate gay people?"**

**The reasoning:** The Church doesn't hate anyone. The Church teaches that same-sex sexual activity is contrary to God's design for sexuality, just as extramarital heterosexual activity is. People with same-sex attraction are called to chastity, not heterosexuality.

**How to respond:** "The Church doesn't hate gay people. The Church teaches that sex is meant for marriage between a man and a woman. Same-sex attracted people, like unmarried heterosexual people, are called to chastity. This is difficult teaching. Many good people struggle with it. The Church is asking people to live against their desires — that's hard. But it's not about hate."

**"Why is premarital sex wrong if we love each other?"**

**The reasoning:** Sex is the seal of a lifelong commitment, not a trial period. It creates deep bonds that are painful to break. It risks creating children without the stability of married parents. It treats a sacred act as recreational.

**How to respond:** "The Church believes sex creates a bond that's meant to last forever. When you have sex with someone, you're saying 'I give myself to you completely' — but without the commitment of marriage, that's not true. You're holding back. Plus, sex can create children. Don't you think children deserve parents who have committed to each other for life?"

### Life Issues Questions

**"Why is abortion wrong?"**

**The reasoning:** Human life begins at conception. The unborn child is a human person with a right to life. Abortion intentionally kills an innocent human being. The circumstances of conception don't change the humanity of the child.

**How to respond:** "The Church believes life begins at conception. The unborn baby is a human person with a right to life. Abortion ends that life. I know there are hard cases — rape, danger to the mother's life, severe fetal abnormalities. The Church acknowledges these difficulties. But the fundamental question is: Is the unborn baby a human person? If yes, then abortion is the taking of innocent human life."

**"What about cases of rape or incest?"**

**The reasoning:** The circumstances of conception don't change the humanity of the child. Two wrongs (the rape and the abortion) don't make a right. The child is innocent. However, the Church acknowledges the profound trauma and supports mothers in these situations.

**How to respond:** "Rape is a terrible evil. The woman is a victim who deserves support and care. But the child conceived is also innocent. The Church asks: Should we solve the trauma of rape by ending an innocent life? The Church says no — but the Church also says we must surround that mother with every support she needs."

**"Why is euthanasia wrong?"**

**The reasoning:** Human life has intrinsic value regardless of utility or suffering. Intentionally killing the innocent is always wrong. Palliative care to relieve suffering is morally good; intentionally causing death is not.

**How to respond:** "The Church believes every human life has value, even when the person is suffering or near death. We can and should relieve suffering through medicine and care. But intentionally causing death — even to end suffering — crosses a line. It's not that the Church wants people to suffer. It's that the Church believes killing is never the solution."

### Gender and Sexuality Questions

**"Why doesn't the Church accept transgender identity?"**

**The reasoning:** The Church believes biological sex is a given reality, not a social construct. Gender identity cannot override biological sex. The body reveals who we are; we don't impose our will on the body.

**How to respond:** "The Church believes our bodies tell us who we are. Male and female are real categories rooted in biology. The Church is concerned that transgender ideology separates identity from the body in ways that may not be healthy. The Church wants to help people who experience gender dysphoria — but it questions whether transitioning is the best approach. This is a complex area where the Church is still developing its response."

**"Why are women excluded from the priesthood?"**

**The reasoning:** The priesthood isn't a right or position of power. It's a calling to act in persona Christi. Jesus chose only men as apostles, and the Church doesn't have authority to change what Christ established. This doesn't mean women are less valuable — Mary is the highest creature.

**How to respond:** "The priesthood isn't about power or rights. It's about acting in the person of Christ. Jesus chose only men as apostles, and the Church believes it can't change that. This doesn't mean women are less important — look at Mary's role. Women have always been central to the Church. But the priesthood is specifically male, not because men are better, but because of how the priest represents Christ."

### Authority and Structure Questions

**"Why do I have to go to confession to a priest? Can't I just confess to God?"**

**The reasoning:** You can confess to God directly — and should, when venial sins are concerned. But for mortal sins, Christ gave the apostles (and their successors) authority to forgive sins. The priest acts in persona Christi.

**How to respond:** "You can always confess directly to God. But Jesus gave His apostles authority to forgive sins — that's in John 20. The priest acts in the person of Christ when he absolves. There's also something powerful about speaking sins aloud and hearing words of forgiveness. It's not that God can't forgive without the priest. It's that Jesus chose to work this way."

**"Why does the Pope have so much power?"**

**The reasoning:** The Pope's authority is service, not domination. He preserves unity and guards the deposit of faith. His authority is limited — he can't change doctrine, only clarify or develop it.

**How to respond:** "The Pope's job is to keep the Church united and faithful to what Jesus taught. He's the servant of the servants of God. He can't just change teachings he doesn't like — his authority is to preserve what was handed down. When the Pope speaks definitively on faith and morals, Catholics believe he's protected from error. But most of what the Pope does is teach, encourage, and govern — not declare new doctrines."

**"Why can't the Church change teachings that are outdated?"**

**The reasoning:** The Church doesn't invent teachings — it preserves what was revealed by Christ. What God has revealed can't change, even if culture changes. The Church can develop understanding of doctrine, but can't contradict it.

**How to respond:** "The Church sees itself as the guardian of what Jesus taught, not the inventor of new teachings. If Jesus really said and did what the Gospels record, then the Church can't change that — even if it's unpopular. The Church can develop its understanding, but it can't contradict what was revealed. That's why some teachings don't change — the Church doesn't have authority to change what God established."

## When Questions Become Rejection

### Recognizing the Shift

Sometimes honest questioning becomes hardened rejection. Watch for:

- Refusal to engage with resources
- Increasing hostility in tone
- Dismissal of all Church authority
- Moral compromise presented as "enlightened"
- "I'm done with the Church"

### Responding to Rejection

**Don't:**
- Argue harder (it doesn't work)
- Shame or guilt-trip
- Cut off relationship
- Accept their rejection as permanent

**Do:**
- Express love unconditionally
- Maintain relationship
- Continue living your faith visibly
- Pray for their return
- Hope for future openness

**What to say:**

"I hear you. I disagree, and I'm sad about this, but I hear you. I hope you'll keep seeking truth, even if it's not with the Church right now. And I hope you'll keep talking to me. I love you regardless of what you believe."

### Books by Topic

**Sexual morality:**
- "Theology of the Body for Beginners" by Christopher West
- "The Meaning of Marriage" by Tim Keller
- "Love and Responsibility" by Karol Wojtyla

**Life issues:**
- "The Unaborted Socrates" by Peter Kreeft
- "Persuasive Pro-Life" by Trent Horn
- "Defending Life" by Francis Beckwith

**Gender and sexuality:**
- "The Third Way" (documentary on same-sex attraction and Catholicism)
- "Male and Female He Created Them" by Pope Francis
- "Irreversible Damage" by Abigail Shrier (on transgender issues)

**Authority and Church structure:**
- "The Lamb's Supper" by Scott Hahn (on the Mass and authority)
- "Upon This Rock" by Scott Hahn (on Peter and the papacy)
- "Catholicism and Fundamentalism" by Karl Keating

### People to Consult

- Your parish priest
- Diocese vocation director or youth minister
- Catholic campus minister (if in college)
- Catholic Answers (apologetics organization)
- Trusted Catholic friends with strong formation

## The Parent's Prayer for a Teen Questioning Teaching

"Lord, my child is questioning the Church's teaching on [topic]. They're not being rebellious — they're honestly trying to understand. Or maybe they are being rebellious, and I can't tell the difference.

Either way, I ask for Your help. Give me wisdom to answer their questions well. Give me patience when I don't know the answers. Give me humility to admit when the teaching is difficult. Give me courage to present the truth even when it's unpopular.

Help my child distinguish between honest questioning and hard-hearted rejection. Give them open minds and soft hearts. Surround them with faithful witnesses who can explain the faith well.

And if they ultimately reject this teaching, don't let them reject You. Keep them in relationship with You, even if they struggle with the Church. Draw them deeper into truth, whatever that takes.

I trust You with my child's faith formation. You are the true Teacher. Let them hear Your voice, even through their questions. Amen."
