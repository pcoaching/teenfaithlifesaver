# Chapter 17: When They Shut Down Completely / When to Get Help
## The Crisis Protocol — Part 5

---

### The Silent Treatment

Sometimes the crisis isn't loud — it's silent. Your teen stops talking. They withdraw. They shut down. They become a stranger living in your home.

**Signs of shutdown:**
- One-word answers to questions
- Spending all time in their room
- Avoiding family meals and activities
- No eye contact
- No sharing about life, friends, or feelings
- Physical presence but emotional absence

**This is often more dangerous than loud rebellion.** At least rebellion is engagement. Shutdown is disconnection. And disconnection kills faith faster than argument.

---

## Understanding the Shutdown

### Possible Causes

**Depression or anxiety:**
Mental illness often manifests as withdrawal. Your teen isn't being difficult — they're suffering.

**Trauma:**
Something happened — abuse, assault, accident, loss — and they can't process it.

**Shame:**
They've done something (or something was done to them) that creates overwhelming shame. They hide to avoid exposure.

**Overwhelm:**
Life feels unmanageable. School, relationships, expectations — it's all too much. Shutdown is escape.

**Identity confusion:**
They don't know who they are. Gender, sexuality, vocation, beliefs — everything is uncertain. Silence hides the confusion.

**Anger too dangerous to express:**
They're furious but fear what will happen if they express it. Silence contains the rage.

**Learned helplessness:**
Previous attempts to communicate were dismissed or punished. They stopped trying.

**Protection from you:**
They perceive you as unsafe — judgmental, reactive, controlling. They hide to protect themselves.

### The Difference Between Normal Teen Withdrawal and Crisis Shutdown

**Normal teen withdrawal:**
- Still has some friends they talk to
- Engages with hobbies or interests
- Occasionally shares (even if brief)
- Maintains basic responsibilities
- Seems generally functional

**Crisis shutdown:**
- Isolated from everyone, not just family
- No interest in previously enjoyed activities
- Complete refusal to communicate
- Neglecting school, hygiene, responsibilities
- Seems distressed, hopeless, or empty

**When in doubt, assume crisis.** Better to intervene unnecessarily than miss a serious problem.

## Staying Connected Through Silence

### Respect the Silence (But Don't Accept It Forever)

**Immediate response:** Give them space. Don't interrogate. Don't fill every silence with words.

**Ongoing response:** Stay present. Be available. Create opportunities for connection without demanding it.

### Practical Ways to Stay Present

**Physical presence:**
- Be in the same room without requiring conversation
- Watch TV or movies together (shared experience without talking)
- Drive them places (car conversations often happen because you're not face-to-face)
- Sit near them while they do homework, you read

**Small gestures:**
- Leave a note: "Thinking of you. Love you."
- Make their favorite food
- Do one of their chores unexpectedly
- Leave a book or article they might like
- Small gifts that say "I see you"

**Consistent routines:**
- Family meals (even if they don't talk)
- Weekly one-on-one time (even if it's silent)
- Bedtime check-ins (even if they grunt)
- Birthday and special day recognition

**Non-verbal communication:**
- Eye contact and smile
- Physical affection (if they'll accept it — pat on shoulder, hug if welcome)
- Listening to music they like
- Being interested in their interests (even silently)

### The Letter Approach

When verbal communication isn't working, write.

**Why letters work:**
- They can't interrupt or argue
- They can read it multiple times
- It bypasses immediate emotional reaction
- It shows effort and care
- It gives them time to respond (or not)

**What to include in a letter:**
- Affirmation of love (unconditional)
- Observation of the silence (without accusation)
- Invitation to talk (without pressure)
- Apology for any part you played in the distance
- Hope for reconnection
- Commitment to keep trying

**Sample letter:**

"Dear [name],

I'm writing because I miss you. I know you're going through something, and I know you don't want to talk about it right now. I respect that. But I need you to know some things.

First, I love you. No matter what. No matter how silent you are, no matter what you're struggling with, no matter what you've done or what's been done to you. I love you completely.

Second, I see that you're hurting. I don't know why, and you don't have to tell me. But I see it. And I'm sorry you're in pain.

Third, I'm sorry if I contributed to this. If I was too harsh, too controlling, too dismissive, too busy — I'm sorry. I'm trying to be better.

Fourth, I'm here. Today, tomorrow, whenever you're ready. I'll keep trying to connect. I'll keep leaving the door open. I'll keep loving you, even in silence.

You don't have to respond to this letter. You don't have to talk to me tomorrow. But please know: I'm not giving up on you. Ever.

Love, [Mom/Dad]"

**After the letter:**
Don't ask if they got it. Don't ask what they thought. Just continue being present. The letter plants a seed. Let it grow.

## Rebuilding Bridges

### When They're Ready to Reconnect

**Signs they're opening up:**
- Longer responses to questions
- Initiating conversation (even briefly)
- Spending time in common areas
- Accepting invitations to activities
- Making eye contact
- Physical affection (initiating or accepting)

**How to receive them:**
- Don't say "I told you so" or "Finally"
- Don't interrogate about the silence
- Don't demand immediate deep conversation
- Receive them with joy and relief (but not desperation)
- Let them set the pace
- Be patient if progress is slow

**Sample response:**
"I'm so glad you're talking to me. I've missed you. No pressure to explain everything — just glad you're here."

### If They Never Fully Reopen

Some teens maintain distance into adulthood. This is heartbreaking, but not uncommon.

**What you can do:**
- Continue reaching out periodically
- Respect their boundaries
- Pray for them
- Hope for future reconciliation
- Find support for your own grief

**What you cannot do:**
- Force relationship
- Control their choices
- Make them explain themselves
- Guarantee reconciliation

## When to Get Outside Help

### Red Lines That Require Professional Intervention

**Mental Health Crisis:**

**Signs:**
- Depression lasting more than two weeks
- Self-harm (cutting, burning)
- Eating disorders (severe restriction, purging, bingeing)
- Suicidal ideation (talking about death, giving away possessions, saying goodbye)
- Severe anxiety or panic attacks
- Psychosis (hearing voices, delusions)

**Action:** Contact a mental health professional immediately. Don't wait. This is beyond what parents can handle alone.

**Resources:**
- Your teen's doctor
- School counselor
- Catholic therapist or counselor
- Crisis hotline: 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline)
- Emergency room (if immediate danger)

**Substance Abuse:**

**Signs:**
- Using drugs or alcohol daily
- Unable to function without substances
- Legal trouble related to substance use
- Health problems from use
- Stealing or lying to obtain substances
- Previous attempts to quit have failed

**Action:** They need treatment. This may mean:
- Outpatient counseling
- Intensive outpatient program
- Residential treatment
- 12-step program

**Don't enable:** Don't give money that could be used for drugs. Don't cover for them. Don't minimize the problem.

**Dangerous Relationships:**

**Signs:**
- Abusive romantic partner (physical, emotional, sexual)
- Cult involvement
- Criminal associations
- Online predators
- Trafficking situations

**Action:** Intervene to protect their safety. This may mean:
- Ending the relationship (if you have legal authority)
- Reporting to authorities
- Removing them from the situation
- Legal action (restraining orders, etc.)

**Academic Failure:**

**Signs:**
- Failing multiple classes
- Academic probation
- Loss of scholarship
- Not attending classes
- Cheating or plagiarism

**Action:** Help them make a plan:
- Tutoring
- Reduced course load
- Academic counseling
- Taking a semester off
- Transferring to a different school
- Alternative educational paths

**Complete Withdrawal:**

**Signs:**
- Stops communicating entirely
- No response to texts, calls, or visits
- Disappears for days or weeks
- Social media goes dark
- Friends and family can't reach them

**Action:** Visit in person. Check on them. If you can't reach them, contact:
- Their friends
- Their school
- Their employer
- Local authorities (if you fear for their safety)

## Types of Professional Help

### Catholic Counselor/Therapist

**Best for:** Mental health issues, family conflict, faith crises, moral struggles

**Why Catholic:** Understands faith framework; won't undermine religious values; can integrate spirituality into therapy

**How to find:**
- CatholicPsych.com (directory of Catholic therapists)
- Ask your parish priest
- Catholic Charities
- Local Catholic colleges (counseling programs)

### Spiritual Director

**Best for:** Faith crises, vocational discernment, prayer difficulties, spiritual dryness

**What they do:** Help discern God's voice, navigate spiritual challenges, deepen prayer life

**Not the same as:** Therapy (doesn't treat mental illness)

**How to find:**
- Ask your parish priest
- Local religious orders
- Diocese vocation office
- Spiritual Directors International (spiritualdirectors.org)

### Priest

**Best for:** Sacramental needs (Confession, spiritual counsel), theological questions, crisis of faith

**What they do:** Offer sacraments, spiritual guidance, prayer, blessing

**Limitations:** Not trained therapists; may not have time for extensive counseling

**How to approach:** Call the parish office; schedule an appointment; be honest about the need

### Family Therapist

**Best for:** Family conflict, communication breakdown, parenting challenges

**What they do:** Help family systems function better; improve communication; resolve conflict

**When needed:** When the whole family is struggling, not just the teen

### Support Groups

**For teens:**
- Depression/anxiety support groups
- Grief support (if they've experienced loss)
- Addiction recovery (AA, NA, Celebrate Recovery)
- LGBTQ+ support (if struggling with identity — choose carefully to ensure it's consistent with faith values)

**For parents:**
- Parents of teens with mental illness
- Parents of addicted children
- Grief support (if you've lost a child to apostasy)
- Empty nest/support groups

## How to Approach Getting Help

### With Your Teen

**If they're open:**
"I think it would help to talk to someone outside our family. A counselor who can give you tools to handle what you're going through. Would you be willing to try?"

**If they're resistant:**
"I know you don't want to do this. But I'm your parent, and I have to make decisions for your wellbeing. We're going to see a counselor. You don't have to like it, but I need you to go."

**If there's immediate danger:**
Don't negotiate. Get help immediately. Their safety trumps their preferences.

### For Yourself

Parents of struggling teens need support too.

**Consider for yourself:**
- Counseling (to process your own grief, anxiety, guilt)
- Spiritual direction (to navigate your own faith challenges)
- Support groups (to know you're not alone)
- Retreats (to recharge spiritually)

**You cannot pour from an empty cup.** Taking care of yourself isn't selfish — it's necessary.

## The Parent's Prayer for a Teen in Crisis

"Lord, my child has shut down. They won't talk to me. They won't let me in. I don't know if they're depressed, angry, ashamed, or just growing up. But I know something is wrong.

I feel helpless. I feel like I've failed. I feel like I'm losing them.

But You are the Good Shepherd. You leave the ninety-nine to find the one lost sheep. You search until You find them. You carry them home on Your shoulders.

Find my child, Lord. Whatever valley they're in, go there. Whatever darkness surrounds them, be light. Whatever silence they've built, pierce it with Your love.

Give me wisdom to know when to push and when to wait. Give me strength to keep loving when I'm rejected. Give me hope when everything seems hopeless.

I surrender my child to Your mercy. You love them more than I do. You see them when I cannot. You can reach them when I cannot.

Bring them home, Lord. In Your time, in Your way, bring them home.

Amen."

## Final Words to Parents

If you're reading this chapter, you're likely in one of the hardest seasons of parenting. Your teen is in crisis — refusing Mass, announcing unbelief, questioning everything, or shutting down completely.

**You are not alone.** Countless parents have walked this road. Many have seen their children return to faith. Some are still waiting. All have learned to trust God in new ways.

**You have not necessarily failed.** Teens have free will. They make their own choices. You can do everything "right" and still face this crisis. You can make mistakes and still see redemption. God's grace is bigger than your parenting.

**Your faithfulness matters.** Continue praying. Continue loving. Continue living your faith visibly. Even if your teen rejects it now, your witness plants seeds that may bear fruit later.

**Your relationship is the bridge.** Whatever happens with faith, maintain relationship. A teen who feels loved and accepted is more likely to return to faith than one who feels judged and rejected.

**God is faithful.** He loves your child more than you do. He is pursuing them even when you can't see it. He can reach them in ways you cannot. Trust Him.

**This is not the end of the story.** Many young people who leave the faith in their teens return in their 20s, 30s, or beyond. The story isn't over until it's over.

**Take care of yourself.** You cannot save your child. You can only love them, pray for them, and trust God. Get support for yourself. Process your grief. Maintain your own spiritual life.

**Hope.** Even in the darkest night, hope. The sun will rise. The prodigal son returned. The lost sheep was found. Your child is not beyond God's reach.

**You are a good parent.** Reading this book proves you care. You're seeking wisdom. You're trying. That's enough. The rest is in God's hands.

---

**Go in peace. Pray without ceasing. Love without limit. Trust without fear.**

**God is with you. God is with your child. All shall be well.**
