# Chapter 15: When They Announce Unbelief
## The Crisis Protocol — Part 5

---

### The Words Every Catholic Parent Fears

"I don't believe in God anymore."

"I'm not Catholic anymore."

"I don't know if I believe any of it."

These words hit like a physical blow. Your stomach drops. Your heart races. Your mind floods with fear, grief, and questions.

**This is a crisis. But it's not necessarily the end.**

How you respond in the next thirty seconds will largely determine whether this is a temporary struggle or a permanent departure. Your reaction matters more than your theology.

---

## The First Response (Critical)

### The Thirty Seconds That Determine Everything

When your teen announces unbelief, what you do immediately is crucial:

**THE BAD RESPONSE (What Not to Do):**

"How can you say that? After everything we've taught you? After everything the Church has given you? This is just a phase. You'll get over it. You're just confused. You don't know what you're saying."

**What this communicates:** Your feelings aren't valid. I don't respect your honesty. I'm going to argue you out of this.

**The result:** Your teen learns that doubts are unsafe to express. They stop talking to you. They take their questions elsewhere — usually to sources that confirm their doubts.

**THE GOOD RESPONSE (What to Do):**

[Pause. Breathe.]

"Thank you for telling me. I know that took courage. I'm not going to panic or argue. I want to understand. When you say you don't believe, what specifically don't you believe? God exists? Jesus is God? The Church has authority? Help me understand where you are."

**What this communicates:** Your honesty is safe with me. I respect you enough to listen. I'm interested in understanding, not just winning.

**The result:** Your teen feels heard and respected. The conversation continues. Trust is preserved (or built). You remain a resource for their spiritual journey.

## Understanding the Announcement

### Types of Unbelief Announcements

**Type 1: Intellectual Doubt**

**What they say:** "I don't think there's good evidence for God." "Evolution disproves creation." "The problem of evil makes belief impossible."

**What's happening:** They've encountered intellectual challenges they can't answer. They assume Christianity has no answers.

**The real issue:** Usually not intellectual — it's emotional or social, with intellectual rationalization. But treat the intellectual questions seriously.

**Type 2: Moral Rejection**

**What they say:** "I can't believe in a God who would [send people to hell/judge LGBTQ people/allow suffering]." "The Church is corrupt/hypocritical/hateful."

**What's happening:** They've encountered a moral teaching or Church failure they can't accept. They reject the faith because of moral objections.

**The real issue:** Often genuine moral sensitivity that hasn't been given adequate theological framework. Sometimes rationalization for wanting to live differently.

**Type 3: Social Pressure**

**What they say:** "None of my friends believe." "Believing makes me weird." "I can't be myself and be Catholic."

**What's happening:** Faith has become socially costly. They're prioritizing belonging over believing.

**The real issue:** Normal adolescent need for belonging. May indicate weak internalization of faith or lack of Catholic community.

**Type 4: Emotional Reaction**

**What they say:** "If God exists, why did [tragedy] happen?" "I prayed and nothing happened." "God abandoned me."

**What's happening:** Suffering, loss, or disappointment has created anger at God. They're not rejecting God intellectually — they're angry at Him.

**The real issue:** Grief, pain, and the problem of suffering. They need accompaniment through darkness, not arguments.

**Type 5: Experimentation/Identity Formation**

**What they say:** "I'm exploring different worldviews." "I need to figure out what I believe on my own." "I don't want to just believe what you believe."

**What's happening:** Normal adolescent identity formation. They're separating from parents and trying on different identities.

**The real issue:** Not necessarily rejection of faith content, but rejection of inherited faith. They need to make faith their own.

## The 30-Day Response Plan

### Week 1: Listen Only

**Goal:** Understand before being understood.

**What to do:**
- Ask clarifying questions
- Listen without defending
- Take notes if helpful
- Thank them for honesty
- Express love unconditionally

**What NOT to do:**
- Argue
- Quote Scripture or Catechism
- Bring up proofs for God's existence
- Tell them they're wrong
- Panic or express despair

**Sample questions:**
- "When did you start feeling this way?"
- "What specifically changed your mind?"
- "Is there a specific event or person that influenced you?"
- "How are you feeling about this?"
- "What do you believe now, if anything?"
- "Have you told anyone else?"

**Your mantra this week:** "I want to understand. Help me understand."

### Week 2: Share Your Own Faith Journey

**Goal:** Normalize struggle and model honest faith.

**What to do:**
- Share your own doubts and struggles
- Tell stories of times you questioned
- Describe how you worked through difficulties
- Be honest about ongoing questions
- Show that faith coexists with uncertainty

**What NOT to do:**
- Use your story to prove them wrong
- Suggest their doubts are less valid than yours were
- Claim you never had serious doubts
- Make it about you instead of them

**Sample sharing:**

"I want you to know that I've had doubts too. When I was [age], I struggled with [specific issue]. I remember feeling [emotion]. What helped me was [process/resource/person]. I still have questions, but I've learned that faith isn't the absence of doubt — it's choosing to believe despite uncertainty."

**Your mantra this week:** "Doubt is part of faith. Let me tell you about my doubts."

### Week 3: Invite Study Together

**Goal:** Make truth-seeking a shared project.

**What to do:**
- Suggest reading a book together
- Watch videos or documentaries together
- Meet with a priest or knowledgeable person together
- Attend a talk or conference together
- Make it collaborative, not confrontational

**What NOT to do:**
- Assign them reading as homework
- Spring a meeting with a priest as an ambush
- Choose resources that are obviously one-sided
- Get frustrated if they're not immediately convinced

**Sample invitation:**

"I know you're not sure what you believe. I'm not trying to change your mind — that's between you and God. But I care about truth, and I care about you. Would you be willing to explore some of these questions together? Not so I can prove you wrong, but so we can both learn. I've always wanted to understand [topic] better myself."

**Resource suggestions by topic:**

**If intellectual/scientific:**
- "The Case for Jesus" by Brant Pitre
- "The Language of God" by Francis Collins (theist scientist)
- Bishop Barron's Word on Fire videos

**If moral/objections to Church teaching:**
- "Theology of the Body for Beginners" by Christopher West
- "The Meaning of Marriage" by Tim Keller
- "The Lamb's Supper" by Scott Hahn (on the Mass)

**If problem of evil/suffering:**
- "The Problem of Pain" by C.S. Lewis
- "Making Sense Out of Suffering" by Peter Kreeft
- "Where Is God When It Hurts?" by Philip Yancee

**If general doubt:**
- "Mere Christianity" by C.S. Lewis
- "Orthodoxy" by G.K. Chesterton
- "The Reason for God" by Tim Keller

**Your mantra this week:** "Let's seek truth together."

### Week 4: Evaluate and Plan

**Goal:** Assess where things stand and determine next steps.

**Questions to consider:**
- Is there openness to continued dialogue?
- Are they willing to engage with resources?
- Is the unbelief intellectual, emotional, or volitional?
- Are there underlying issues (depression, influence of concerning friends, trauma)?
- Do they need professional help (counselor, spiritual director)?

**Possible outcomes:**

**Outcome A: They're open and engaged.**
Continue the process. Keep studying together. Be patient. Faith reconstruction takes time.

**Outcome B: They're closed but relationship is intact.**
Back off from direct religious conversation. Focus on relationship. Pray. Wait for them to initiate.

**Outcome C: They're closed and relationship is damaged.**
Seek family counseling. There are deeper issues than faith that need addressing.

**Outcome D: There are serious underlying issues (depression, abuse, addiction).**
Seek professional help immediately. Faith questions may be symptoms of larger problems.

## Rebuilding Connection Regardless of Faith

### The Principle: Love Is Non-Negotiable

Your teen's faith may be uncertain. Your love must not be.

**What this looks like:**
- Normal family activities continue
- Affection is expressed unchanged
- They remain part of family life
- You don't treat them as "the lost child"
- You don't compare them to believing siblings

**What this doesn't mean:**
- You pretend you're fine with their unbelief (you're not, and that's okay)
- You never talk about faith (you can, gently)
- You change your own practice (continue living your faith)

### Practical Ways to Maintain Connection

**Shared activities unrelated to faith:**
- Sports or exercise together
- Movies or shows (discuss themes, not just "is it appropriate")
- Meals at favorite restaurants
- Hobbies and interests you share
- Travel or day trips

**Respectful dialogue about faith:**
- "I know you're questioning. Can I tell you what I believe and why? You don't have to agree — I just want you to understand me."
- "I'm curious — what do you think happens after death? What gives life meaning?"
- "I respect your honesty. I disagree, but I respect that you're thinking seriously."

**Honest expression of your feelings:**
- "I won't lie — I'm sad about this. I believe faith is the most important thing, and I want that for you. But I'm also glad you trust me enough to be honest."
- "I'm praying for you. I know you may not want that, but I am. I can't not pray for my child."

## When They Come Back (Or If They Don't)

### If They Return to Faith

**Do:**
- Celebrate privately (not "I told you so")
- Welcome them without interrogation
- Let them own their return
- Support their reintegration into practice
- Thank God in prayer

**Don't:**
- Ask "what convinced you?" (they may not know)
- Expect immediate perfect practice
- Hold their past doubts against them
- Tell everyone (respect their privacy)

### If They Don't Return (Yet)

**Do:**
- Continue loving them unconditionally
- Maintain relationship
- Pray persistently
- Hope for future return (many do)
- Trust God's timing and mercy

**Don't:**
- Cut them off
- Give up on them
- Assume they're "lost" eternally (you don't know)
- Let their unbelief destroy your own faith

## The Parent's Prayer for a Teen in Unbelief

"Lord, my child has told me they don't believe. My heart is broken. I feel like I've failed, like everything I taught them was rejected, like I'm watching them walk away from the most important thing in life.

But I know You love them more than I do. You created them. You died for them. You're pursuing them even now, in ways I can't see.

So I surrender my child to Your mercy. Do whatever it takes to bring them home. If it takes years, be patient with them. If it takes suffering, use it to draw them to You. If it takes a dramatic encounter, provide it.

Give me the grace to love them unconditionally while maintaining my own faith. Give me wisdom to know when to speak and when to be silent. Give me patience to wait for Your timing.

I place my child in the arms of Mary, who never loses a child. She prayed for her Son on the Cross when He seemed defeated. She'll pray for my child now.

I believe, Lord. Help my unbelief. And help my child believe again. Amen."
