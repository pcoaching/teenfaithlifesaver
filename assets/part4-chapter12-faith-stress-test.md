# Chapter 12: The Faith Stress Test
## The College Transition — Part 4

---

### What Is the Faith Stress Test?

Before your teen leaves for college, they should undergo a comprehensive assessment — the Faith Stress Test. This evaluates their readiness across five dimensions: intellectual, social, moral, emotional, and practical.

The test isn't about achieving perfection. It's about identifying gaps that need addressing before your teen faces college pressure. A teen who fails the stress test isn't ready for college — at least not secular college. They need more formation first.

Think of it like a physical before sports participation. You wouldn't send an athlete onto the field with an undiagnosed heart condition. Don't send your teen into college with undiagnosed faith vulnerabilities.

---

## SECTION 1: INTELLECTUAL READINESS

### Can Your Teen Articulate Why They Believe?

Not just "because my parents do" or "because the Church says so." Can they give reasons? Can they respond to common objections? Do they know that Christianity is intellectually respectable?

### The Intellectual Readiness Assessment

**Test Questions — Ask Your Teen:**

**Question 1: Why do you believe God exists?**

Good answers include:
- The universe had a beginning (cosmological argument)
- The universe is finely tuned for life (teleological argument)
- Objective morality exists (moral argument)
- Millions of people have experienced God
- Personal experience of God's presence

Concerning answers:
- "I don't know"
- "Because my parents believe"
- "I just do"
- "I don't really think about it"

**Question 2: Why do you believe Jesus rose from the dead?**

Good answers include:
- The Gospels are reliable historical documents
- The apostles died for their testimony
- The resurrection explains the origin of Christianity
- The empty tomb is historically attested

Concerning answers:
- "The Bible says so"
- "I just have faith"
- "Does it matter?"
- "Maybe He didn't"

**Question 3: Why are you Catholic rather than Protestant or nothing?**

Good answers include:
- The Eucharist — the Real Presence
- The authority of the Church founded by Christ
- The sacraments
- Sacred Tradition and Scripture together
- Historical continuity with the apostles

Concerning answers:
- "I was born Catholic"
- "I don't know the difference"
- "They're all the same"
- "I'm not sure I want to be Catholic"

**Question 4: How would you respond to someone who says science disproves religion?**

Good answers include:
- Science and faith answer different questions
- Many great scientists were Catholics
- The Church founded universities and supported science
- Evolution doesn't disprove God

Concerning answers:
- Silence
- "I guess science is right"
- "You just have to have faith"
- "I don't know"

**Question 5: How would you respond to someone who says all religions are the same?**

Good answers include:
- They make contradictory claims — they can't all be true
- Christianity claims Jesus is God — other religions don't
- The differences matter

Concerning answers:
- "Maybe they're all true in their own way"
- "As long as you're a good person"
- "I don't want to judge"

**Question 6: How would you respond to someone who says the Church hates women/gays?**

Good answers include:
- The Church teaches that certain actions don't lead to human flourishing
- This isn't about hate — it's about truth and love
- The Church has a positive vision of sexuality (Theology of the Body)
- Many women and same-sex attracted people find fulfillment in the Church

Concerning answers:
- "I know, it's terrible"
- "I don't agree with that either"
- "The Church is wrong"

### If Your Teen Can't Answer These

They need pre-college apologetics training. Consider:

**Reading:**
- "The Case for Jesus" by Brant Pitre
- "The Handbook of Catholic Apologetics" by Peter Kreeft and Ronald Tacelli
- "Mere Christianity" by C.S. Lewis
- "Theology for Beginners" by Frank Sheed

**Watching:**
- Bishop Barron's Word on Fire videos (YouTube)
- Catholic Answers Live (podcast/YouTube)
- The Case for Christ documentary

**Doing:**
- Online apologetics course (Catholic Distance University, Homeschool Connections)
- Meeting with a knowledgeable priest or mentor
- Starting a study group with other Catholic teens

**Key Arguments to Know Before College:**

**The Cosmological Argument (First Cause):**
- Whatever begins to exist has a cause
- The universe began to exist (Big Bang)
- Therefore, the universe has a cause
- That cause is God

**The Moral Argument:**
- Objective moral values exist (torturing babies is wrong everywhere, always)
- If God doesn't exist, objective moral values don't exist
- Therefore, God exists

**The Historical Evidence for the Resurrection:**
- Jesus died by crucifixion
- His disciples believed He rose and appeared to them
- The disciples were transformed from fearful to bold witnesses
- James (skeptic) and Paul (persecutor) converted
- The tomb was empty
- The best explanation is that Jesus actually rose

**Why Catholicism:**
- Jesus founded a Church on Peter (Matthew 16)
- That Church has continued for 2,000 years
- The Catholic Church is that Church
- The Eucharist is the Real Presence (John 6)
- The Church has authority to teach (Matthew 18, 1 Timothy 3)

## SECTION 2: SOCIAL READINESS

### Can Your Teen Handle Pressure to Conform?

College social pressure is relentless. Can your teen say no to drinking, drugs, and hookups when everyone else is saying yes? Can they be different without being obnoxious? Can they find friends who share their values?

### Role-Play Scenarios

Practice these conversations before college:

**Scenario 1: Drinking Pressure**

Friend: "Come on, everyone's drinking. Don't be boring."

Weak response: "Um, I don't know... I guess maybe..."

Strong response: "I'm good, thanks. I don't drink. But I'll hang out — I'm not judging anyone who does. It's just not for me."

**Scenario 2: Hookup Culture**

Friend: "You don't hook up? What's wrong with you?"

Weak response: "I don't know, I just... it's complicated..."

Strong response: "I believe sex is for marriage. I know that's not popular, but it's what I believe. I'm not judging anyone else — that's just my line."

**Scenario 3: Faith Mockery**

Friend: "You actually believe that religious stuff?"

Weak response: "Well, I mean, sort of, I guess..."

Strong response: "Yeah, I do. I know it's not popular, but it's important to me. I'm happy to talk about it if you're actually curious."

**Scenario 4: Isolation**

Friend: "We're all going to the party. You coming?"

Weak response: "No, I have to... uh... study..."

Strong response: "Not this time. But let's do something else this weekend — coffee or a movie?"

**Scenario 5: Drug Pressure**

Friend: "You smoke? Everyone smokes now."

Weak response: "I've never tried it..."

Strong response: "I don't. It's not my thing. But hey, what class are you taking next semester?"

### The Goal

The goal isn't to win debates. It's to maintain integrity without alienating everyone. Your teen needs to know they can say no and survive socially.

**Key Principles:**

**Be confident but not preachy.** State your position clearly without condemning others.

**Have alternative suggestions.** Don't just refuse — propose something else.

**Use humor when appropriate.** Deflection can work: "Nah, I'm boring like that. So what are you majoring in?"

**Know when to walk away.** Some people won't respect boundaries. Your teen needs permission to distance themselves from those people.

**Find allies.** Even in secular colleges, there are other students who share values. Your teen needs to find them.

## SECTION 3: MORAL READINESS

### Does Your Teen Know Their Boundaries?

Deciding moral questions in the moment is deciding under pressure. Your teen should have clear lines drawn before they face temptation.

### Questions to Answer Before College

**Alcohol:**

- Will you drink at all?
- If yes, how much? What's your limit?
- Will you drink underage?
- Will you drink at parties where you don't know people?
- Will you drink when you need to drive?
- What will you do if someone pressures you to drink more?

**Drugs:**

- What's your stance on marijuana?
- What about prescription drug abuse?
- What if "everyone is doing it"?
- What if it's "just once"?

**Sexuality:**

- What's your stance on hookups?
- What about dating? How far is too far?
- How will you handle a roommate who is sexually active?
- How will you handle pressure regarding gender/sexuality questions?
- What will you do if you're attracted to someone who doesn't share your values?

**Academic Integrity:**

- What's your stance on cheating?
- What about plagiarism?
- What about "collaboration" that crosses the line?
- What if "everyone does it"?
- What if the professor is terrible and "deserves it"?

**Other Boundaries:**

- What time will you go to bed on weeknights?
- How will you manage money?
- What social media boundaries will you keep?
- How will you handle a roommate who violates your boundaries?

### Write These Down

Make your teen articulate their boundaries. Write them down. Sign them. This makes them real and memorable.

**Sample Boundary Statement:**

"Before going to college, I commit to the following boundaries:

1. I will not drink alcohol until I am 21.
2. I will not use drugs of any kind.
3. I will not engage in sexual activity outside of marriage.
4. I will not cheat or plagiarize academically.
5. I will attend Mass every Sunday.
6. I will pray daily.
7. I will maintain a curfew of midnight on weekends.
8. I will call my parents weekly.

I understand these boundaries protect my faith, my health, and my future. If I struggle to maintain them, I will seek help rather than hide my failures."

Signed: _________________ Date: _________________

### Role-Play Boundary Violations

Practice what your teen will do when boundaries are tested:

**Someone offers you a drink:**
"No thanks, I don't drink. But I'll take a soda if they have one."

**Someone offers you drugs:**
"No, I'm not into that. Hey, I'm going to head out. See you later."

**Someone makes a sexual advance:**
"I'm not interested in that. I think you're great, but I'm waiting for marriage."

**A roommate is sexually active in the room:**
"I need to talk to you about something. I respect your choices, but I don't want sexual activity happening in our shared space. Can we talk about how to handle this?"

## SECTION 4: EMOTIONAL READINESS

### Can Your Teen Handle Loneliness, Failure, and Stress Without Abandoning Faith?

College is emotionally overwhelming. Homesickness. Academic pressure. Relationship drama. Identity questions. Many teens abandon faith not because they've stopped believing intellectually, but because they're suffering and feel God has abandoned them.

### Emotional Skills Needed

**Resilience:**

The ability to bounce back from failure. Can your teen fail a test, lose a relationship, or face rejection without falling apart?

Signs of resilience:
- Acknowledging failure without catastrophic thinking
- Learning from mistakes
- Trying again after setbacks
- Maintaining perspective (one failure doesn't define them)

Signs of fragility:
- Catastrophizing ("I failed this test, I'll fail out of school")
- Giving up after one failure
- Blaming others or circumstances exclusively
- Depression or anxiety after setbacks

**Emotional Regulation:**

The ability to manage anxiety, anger, and sadness without destructive behavior. Can your teen handle stress without drinking, hooking up, or shutting down?

Healthy regulation:
- Talking about feelings
- Exercise or physical activity
- Prayer or meditation
- Creative expression
- Seeking support

Unhealthy regulation:
- Substance use
- Sexual acting out
- Isolation
- Self-harm
- Aggression

**Suffering with Christ:**

Understanding that suffering has meaning and that Christ suffers with us. Many teens think suffering means God doesn't exist or doesn't care. They need a theology of the Cross.

Key truths:
- Suffering is not a sign of God's absence
- Christ entered into our suffering
- Suffering can be redemptive when united to Christ
- The Cross precedes the Resurrection

**Help-Seeking:**

The willingness to ask for help when struggling. Many teens (especially boys) suffer in silence until they break.

Signs of healthy help-seeking:
- Talking to parents about struggles
- Using campus counseling services
- Confiding in trusted friends
- Seeing a priest or spiritual director

Signs of unhealthy isolation:
- Hiding struggles from everyone
- Pretending everything is fine
- Refusing support when offered
- Believing they must handle everything alone

### Building Emotional Readiness

**Normalize struggle:**
"College is hard. Everyone struggles. It's normal to feel overwhelmed sometimes."

**Teach the Examen:**
Daily reflection builds self-awareness. See Part 3 for instructions.

**Encourage counseling:**
"Seeing a counselor is smart, not weak. It's like going to the doctor when you're sick."

**Stay connected:**
Loneliness is the enemy; relationship is the defense. Maintain communication.

**Model vulnerability:**
Share your own struggles and how you handle them. Show that adults also need help sometimes.

## SECTION 5: PRACTICAL READINESS

### Does Your Teen Have a Plan for Practicing Faith in College?

Good intentions aren't enough. Your teen needs a concrete plan.

### The Practical Readiness Checklist

**Mass:**

- [ ] Know the location of the nearest Catholic church or campus ministry
- [ ] Know the Mass schedule
- [ ] Know how to get there (walking, bus, car)
- [ ] Have a plan for who to go with (or go alone confidently)
- [ ] Have Sunday Mass scheduled as a non-negotiable

**Prayer:**

- [ ] Decide when to pray daily (morning, evening, or both)
- [ ] Decide where to pray (dorm room, chapel, outdoors)
- [ ] Decide what to pray (Rosary, Liturgy of the Hours, personal prayer)
- [ ] Bring prayer resources (Rosary, prayer book, Bible, apps)

**Catholic Community:**

- [ ] Research the Catholic student center
- [ ] Know when activities are
- [ ] Plan to attend events in the first month
- [ ] Meet the chaplain
- [ ] Connect with other Catholic students

**Confession:**

- [ ] Know where confession is offered
- [ ] Know the schedule
- [ ] Plan how to get there
- [ ] Commit to regular confession (monthly recommended)

**Catholic Friends:**

- [ ] Plan how to meet other Catholic students
- [ ] Join the Catholic student group
- [ ] Attend Catholic events
- [ ] Be intentional about friendship formation

**Sabbath:**

- [ ] Plan how to keep Sunday as the Lord's Day
- [ ] Schedule rest and spiritual activities
- [ ] Avoid homework on Sunday if possible
- [ ] Make Sunday different from other days

### Research Together Before They Leave

Visit the Catholic student center if possible. Meet the chaplain. Know the Mass schedule. Having a plan dramatically increases the likelihood of follow-through.

**Questions to Ask the Campus Chaplain:**

- When is Mass offered?
- What other activities do you have?
- How many students typically attend?
- Is there a Bible study or small group?
- Is there a mentorship program?
- How can my student get involved?

## THE GAP YEAR OPTION

If your teen fails the Faith Stress Test, consider a gap year. A year of formation, work, or service can make the difference between surviving college and losing faith.

### Gap Year Options

**Service programs:**
- NET Ministries (domestic)
- City Year (urban service)
- AmeriCorps (national service)
- Catholic Volunteer Network (various programs)

These provide structure, Catholic community, and time to mature before college.

**Mission work:**
- Domestic missions (Catholic Mission Trips)
- International missions (Maryknoll, Catholic Relief Services)
- Parish-based service

**Work and save:**
Living at home, working, saving money. Less glamorous but provides maturity and financial preparation.

**Community college:**
Living at home while attending community college. Cheaper, less social pressure, continued family support.

**Formation programs:**
- Bible schools
- Discernment houses
- Gap year programs focused on faith formation

**Discernment of religious life:**
If your teen is considering priesthood or religious life, a gap year of discernment is wise before committing to college.

## WARNING SIGNS YOUR TEEN ISN'T READY

Consider delaying college if your teen:

- [ ] Cannot articulate why they believe in God
- [ ] Has no Catholic peer group and shows no interest in finding one
- [ ] Is currently rebelling against faith and family
- [ ] Struggles with substance abuse or other serious issues
- [ ] Has severe anxiety or depression that isn't managed
- [ ] Is desperate to get away from family (indicating unresolved issues)
- [ ] Has no clear boundaries regarding morality
- [ ] Shows no maturity in decision-making

College doesn't fix these problems. It amplifies them. Address them first.

Surround them with good friends and faithful mentors. Place obstacles in the path of harmful choices. Give them the courage to be different, the wisdom to know when to speak and when to stay silent, and the grace to return to You quickly if they stray.

I release them into Your hands. You love them more than I do. Keep them in the faith. Amen."
