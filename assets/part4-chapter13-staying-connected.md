# Chapter 13: Staying Connected Without Hovering
## The College Transition — Part 4

---

### The Tightrope of College Parenting

College parenting requires walking a tightrope. Too distant, and your teen feels abandoned. Too present, and they feel smothered. Too controlling, and they rebel. Too permissive, and they drift.

The goal is strategic connection: present enough to provide anchor, distant enough to allow growth.

This chapter gives you the practical tools to maintain relationship through the college years without becoming a helicopter parent or a disconnected bystander.

---

## THE COMMUNICATION SCHEDULE

### Freshman Year: More Frequent Contact

Freshmen need more support as they adjust. Aim for:

**Weekly Scheduled Call**

Same day, same time. Sunday evenings work well. This isn't interrogation — it's genuine conversation about life, struggles, and faith.

Keep it to 30-45 minutes. Respect their time. If they need to go after 20 minutes, let them. But make the invitation to talk longer.

**Sample Opening:**
"Hey, how was your week? Tell me about the good, the bad, and the ugly."

**Topics to Cover:**
- Classes and academic life
- Friends and social life
- Faith practice (indirectly — "How are you finding God this week?")
- Challenges and struggles
- Victories and joys

**Topics to Avoid:**
- Interrogation about Mass attendance
- Lectures about behavior
- Comparisons to siblings or other students
- Nostalgia that makes them feel guilty

**Daily Availability**

They can text or call anytime. You won't always answer immediately, but you'll respond within the day. Let them know you're available for emergencies.

**Text Ideas:**
- "Praying for your test today!"
- "Saw this and thought of you [article, meme, photo]"
- "How are you doing? No need to respond if you're busy — just thinking of you"
- "Love you. Here if you need anything"

Don't expect immediate responses. They're busy. The message is: "I'm here when you need me."

**Monthly Care Package**

Physical reminders of home and love. Include:

- Homemade or favorite snacks
- Catholic items (prayer cards, holy water, medal, book)
- Handwritten note of encouragement
- Photos from home
- Gift cards for coffee, food, or Amazon
- Seasonal items (Advent calendar, Lenten devotional)

**Sample Care Package Themes:**

September: "Back to School" — snacks, school supplies, prayer for studies
October: "Fall & Faith" — hot cocoa, Rosary, note about Mary's month
November: "Gratitude" — journal, thank-you cards, list of things you appreciate about them
December: "Advent" — Advent calendar, Christmas treats, family photo
January: "New Year" — planner, devotional, goals worksheet
February: "Lent" — Lenten devotional, Stations of the Cross booklet, sacrifice suggestions
March: "Spring" — Easter candy (after Lent), outdoor items, encouragement for final stretch
April: "Finals" — study snacks, coffee, prayer for exams

**Semester Visits**

Visit campus at least once per semester. See their world. Meet their friends. Attend Mass with them. Take them to dinner. Show interest without taking over.

**During Visits:**
- Let them lead — it's their world now
- Meet their friends if possible
- Attend Mass together
- Take them to a nice meal
- Don't interrogate — just enjoy their company
- Bring items they need (saves them money)
- Give them space if they need it

### Sophomore Year and Beyond: Gradual Independence

As your teen matures, gradually reduce contact:

**Communication Changes:**
- Biweekly calls instead of weekly
- Care packages every other month
- Visits once per semester (or they visit you)
- Let them initiate more contact

This isn't abandonment. It's appropriate development. Your goal is an adult relationship, not perpetual childhood.

**Signs They're Ready for More Independence:**
- They initiate contact regularly
- They're handling academics well
- They have established friend groups
- They're practicing faith independently
- They're managing money responsibly

**Signs They Still Need More Support:**
- They rarely initiate contact
- They're struggling academically
- They seem isolated or depressed
- They've stopped practicing faith
- They're making concerning choices

Adjust based on their needs, not just their year.

## WHAT TO ASK (AND NOT ASK)

### Questions That Build Connection

**"How are you really doing?"**

Open-ended, invites honesty. Don't settle for "fine." Ask with genuine interest and wait for a real answer.

**"What are you enjoying most?"**

Positive focus. College has good parts — help them notice and appreciate them.

**"What are you struggling with?"**

Invites vulnerability. Don't panic when they share struggles. Just listen.

**"Where are you finding God?"**

Spiritual check-in without interrogation. This assumes they're looking — and they should be.

**"How can I pray for you?"**

Practical support. Then actually pray — and tell them you did.

**"What do you need?"**

How to help. Sometimes it's money. Sometimes it's advice. Sometimes it's just listening.

### Questions That Damage Connection

**"Are you going to Mass?"**

They'll lie if they're not. Creates defensiveness. Makes faith feel like surveillance.

**Better:** "How's your spiritual life?" or "Where are you finding God?"

**"Are you drinking/partying?"**

Sounds like interrogation. Puts them on the defensive.

**Better:** "How are you handling the social scene?" or "Are you finding good friends?"

**"Have you met someone?"**

Creates pressure about dating. Makes them feel like you're more interested in their relationship status than their wellbeing.

**Better:** Let them share about relationships if they want to. Don't pry.

**"Are you keeping your faith?"**

Anxiety-inducing. Sounds like you're waiting for them to fail.

**Better:** "How's your relationship with God?" or "What's your church community like?"

**"What are your grades?"**

Reduces relationship to performance. Makes them feel like a report card, not a person.

**Better:** "How are classes going?" ( broader, less grade-focused)

### The Principle

Instead of checking religious compliance, nurture relationship. Compliance without relationship is fragile. Relationship sustains faith through struggle.

## LISTENING WITHOUT FIXING

### The Parental Instinct to Fix

When your teen shares struggles, your instinct is to help. You want to solve the problem, offer advice, make it better. Resist this instinct.

Often they don't want solutions — they want to be heard. They're not asking you to fix it. They're asking you to care.

### Bad Response vs. Good Response

**Bad Response — The Fixer:**

Teen: "I'm really struggling with my roommate. We don't get along at all."

Parent: "Here's what you need to do. First, have a conversation about boundaries. Second, if that doesn't work, talk to your RA. Third, request a room change. Let me know if you need me to call someone."

**Result:** Teen feels unheard, managed, and incompetent. They stop sharing struggles because they get lectures instead of support.

**Good Response — The Listener:**

Teen: "I'm really struggling with my roommate. We don't get along at all."

Parent: "That sounds really hard. Tell me more about what's going on."

[Listen. Ask questions. Understand.]

"What do you think you might do?"

[Let them think through solutions.]

"Would you like my input, or do you just need to vent?"

[Ask permission to advise.]

**Result:** Teen feels heard, supported, and capable. They continue sharing struggles because they know you'll listen.

### The Listening Framework

**Step 1: Listen Without Interrupting**

Let them tell the whole story. Don't jump in with solutions. Don't relate it to your own experience (yet). Just listen.

**Step 2: Ask Clarifying Questions**

"Help me understand..."
"What happened next?"
"How did that make you feel?"
"What did you do?"

**Step 3: Reflect Back**

"So what I'm hearing is..."
"It sounds like you're feeling..."
"Correct me if I'm wrong, but..."

**Step 4: Ask What They Need**

"What would be most helpful right now?"
"Do you want advice, or do you just need to talk?"
"How can I support you?"

**Step 5: Offer Input (If Asked)**

"Would you like my perspective?"
"I have some thoughts if you're open to them."
"Can I share what I've seen work?"

**Step 6: Pray Together**

"Can we pray about this?"
Even if they say no, tell them you'll pray for them — and do it.

### When to Offer Unsolicited Advice

Sometimes you need to intervene even if they don't ask:

- Safety concerns (abuse, addiction, self-harm)
- Moral crisis (cheating, stealing, serious sin)
- Mental health crisis (depression, anxiety, eating disorders)
- Academic failure (failing out of school)

Even then, lead with listening and love. Then offer clear guidance.

## WARNING SIGNS FROM A DISTANCE

### Communication Changes

Watch for shifts in how your teen communicates:

**Sudden decrease in contact:**
- They used to call weekly, now it's monthly
- They don't respond to texts for days
- They seem annoyed when you call

**Possible causes:**
- Busy with activities (good)
- New relationship taking time (neutral)
- Avoiding you because of guilt (concerning)
- Depression or isolation (serious)

**Avoiding questions about friends or activities:**
- Vague answers about who they're with
- Changing subjects when you ask about weekend plans
- Defensiveness about social life

**Possible causes:**
- Desire for privacy (normal)
- Friends you wouldn't approve of (concerning)
- Activities they're ashamed of (serious)

**Only calling when they need money:**
- No relationship, just transactions
- They don't share life updates
- Conversations are brief and focused on needs

**Possible causes:**
- Immaturity (they may not realize it's hurtful)
- Distance in relationship (needs repair)
- Using you as an ATM (needs boundaries)

### Behavioral Changes

**Mention of new friends who concern you:**
- "My friend Jake is always high, but he's cool"
- "My roommate hooks up with a different person every weekend"
- "I met this guy who says religion is stupid"

**Response:** Ask questions. "How do you feel about that?" "Do you think that influences you?" Don't forbid the friendship — that backfires. Help them think critically.

**Dropping Catholic activities they previously enjoyed:**
- Stopped going to the Catholic center
- Quit the pro-life group
- No longer mentions prayer or faith

**Response:** Ask indirectly. "How's your church community?" "Are you finding time for prayer?" Express concern without accusation.

**Moral compromise presented as maturity:**
- "Everyone drinks in college — it's just part of growing up"
- "Hooking up is normal now — you wouldn't understand"
- "The Church is wrong about [issue] — I'm more enlightened now"

**Response:** This is serious. They're rationalizing sin. Have a direct conversation. "I'm concerned about what I'm hearing. Can we talk about this?"

**Significant weight change (gain or loss):**
- Could indicate eating disorders, depression, or substance abuse

**Response:** Express concern lovingly. "I've noticed you look different. Are you okay? Is something going on?"

**Sleep problems:**
- Sleeping all day, up all night
- Chronic fatigue
- Insomnia

**Response:** Could be depression, anxiety, or poor habits. Ask about it.

### Academic Changes

**Failing grades (when they were previously good):**
- Could indicate depression, substance abuse, relationship problems, or poor choices

**Response:** "I'm concerned about your grades. What's going on? How can we help?"

**Changing major repeatedly:**
- Could indicate lack of direction, anxiety about future, or external pressure

**Response:** "I want to understand your thought process. Let's talk through this."

**Wanting to drop out:**
- Could indicate serious problems or could be the right decision

**Response:** Don't panic. Listen. Understand. Then help them think through options.

### Spiritual Changes

**Refusing to discuss faith:**
- Changes subject when faith comes up
- "That's private" or "I don't want to talk about it"

**Response:** Respect boundaries but express concern. "I notice you don't want to talk about faith. I'm concerned because it's important to me. Is something going on?"

**Mocking religion:**
- Making fun of Church teaching
- "The Church is stupid"
- "Only idiots believe that"

**Response:** Serious sign. They're distancing themselves from faith. Have a direct conversation.

**New hostility toward the Church:**
- Angry about Church teaching
- "The Church hates women/gays"
- "The Church is corrupt"

**Response:** Could be intellectual doubts or moral rationalization. Engage with the arguments while addressing the heart.

**"Coming out" as atheist or agnostic:**
- Direct statement of unbelief

**Response:** See Part 5, Chapter 15. Don't panic. Listen. This is serious but not hopeless.

### Addressing Concerns

**Don't accuse. Observe and invite conversation:**

"I've noticed [specific observation]. I'm concerned because I love you. How are you really doing?"

**Examples:**

"I've noticed you haven't mentioned church in a while. I'm concerned because faith has always been important to our family. How are you doing spiritually?"

"I've noticed you sound different when you talk about your friends. I'm concerned because I want you to have good influences. How are things going socially?"

"I've noticed your grades dropped this semester. I'm concerned because I know you're capable of more. What's going on?"

**If they open up:** Listen. Don't lecture. Express love. Offer support.

**If they don't:** Express your love and leave the door open. "I'm here when you're ready to talk. I love you no matter what."

## WHEN TO INTERVENE

### Most College Struggles Are Normal

Most college struggles — homesickness, academic stress, relationship drama, identity questions — are normal and should be handled with support, not intervention. Your teen needs to learn to navigate these challenges.

### Situations Requiring Parental Intervention

**Mental Health Crisis:**

Signs: Depression, self-harm, eating disorders, suicidal ideation, severe anxiety

Action: Contact campus counseling services immediately. Consider visiting. Don't wait.

**Substance Abuse:**

Signs: Addiction to drugs or alcohol, inability to function, legal trouble, health problems

Action: They need treatment. This may mean leaving school for rehab. Don't enable.

**Dangerous Relationships:**

Signs: Abusive romantic partner, cult involvement, criminal associations

Action: Intervene to protect their safety. May need to pull them from school.

**Academic Failure:**

Signs: Failing out of school, academic probation, loss of scholarship

Action: Help them make a plan — tutoring, reduced course load, or taking a semester off.

**Complete Withdrawal:**

Signs: Stops communicating entirely, no response to texts/calls for weeks

Action: Visit campus. Something is seriously wrong.

### How to Intervene

**Step 1: Express Concern Lovingly**

"I love you, and I'm concerned about [specific behavior]. I think we need to address this together."

**Step 2: Listen to Their Perspective**

They may have a different view of the situation. Hear them out.

**Step 3: Seek Professional Help**

Don't try to handle serious issues alone. Involve:
- Campus counseling services
- Their doctor
- A priest or spiritual director
- A therapist or counselor
- Academic advisors

**Step 4: Make a Plan Together**

What steps will they take? What support will they accept? What boundaries need to be set?

**Step 5: Follow Up**

Check in regularly. Hold them accountable. Adjust the plan as needed.

**Step 6: Be Willing to Make Hard Decisions**

Sometimes the right decision is pulling them from school, cutting off financial support, or requiring treatment. These are last resorts, but they may be necessary.

## TRUSTING THE PROCESS

### You Cannot Control Your College Student

Ultimately, you cannot control your college student's faith. They're adults (or nearly so). They make their own choices. Your job is to love them, stay connected, and pray — not to manage their spiritual life.

### Some Teens Will Drift

Some teens will drift in college and return later. This is common. The 20s are a time of exploration and questioning. Many who leave the faith in college return in their 30s when they marry and have children.

### Some Will Keep the Faith

Some will keep the faith despite struggles. They'll find Catholic community, persevere through doubt, and emerge stronger.

### Some Will Abandon It

Some will abandon faith despite your best efforts. This is heartbreaking. But it's not necessarily permanent — and it's not your failure. They have free will.

### Trust God

Trust God with your child. He's their Father too. He loves them more than you do. And He's pursuing them even when you can't see it.

**The Parent's Prayer for College Kids:**

"Lord, I release my child into Your hands. You love them more than I do. You see them when I cannot. You pursue them when I cannot reach them. Protect them from harm, error, and evil. Surround them with good friends and faithful mentors. Keep their hearts turned toward You. And give me peace, trusting that You are working even when I cannot see it. Amen."

## FINAL THOUGHTS

College parenting is a season of letting go while staying connected. It's watching your child become an adult while remaining their parent. It's trusting God with the person you love most.

You won't do it perfectly. You'll call too much or too little. You'll say the wrong thing. You'll worry too much. That's okay.

What matters is that your teen knows you love them, that you're there when they need you, and that you're praying for them always.

The rest is in God's hands.
