# Chapter 14: When They Refuse Mass
## The Crisis Protocol — Part 5

---

### Understanding the Refusal

When your teen refuses to go to Mass, it's not random rebellion. It's a symptom of something deeper happening in their heart, mind, or life. Your job is to diagnose before treating.

**Refusing Mass is a red flag, not the disease itself.**

Think of it like a fever. The fever isn't the problem — it's a sign of infection. Treating only the fever (forcing Mass attendance) while ignoring the infection (the underlying issue) doesn't solve the problem and may make it worse.

---

## Possible Causes of Mass Refusal

### 1. Anger at God or the Church

**Signs:**
- Specific grievance mentioned ("After what the Church did...")
- Generalized hostility ("I hate the Church")
- Connection to scandal, abuse, or hypocrisy they've learned about
- Anger at God for suffering, loss, or unanswered prayer

**What's happening:** Your teen has encountered something that shattered their trust or created cognitive dissonance. They can't reconcile what they believed with what they've discovered. Anger is a defense against hurt and confusion.

**Response approach:** Listen to the grievance without defending immediately. Validate the pain. "What you're describing is terrible. I understand why you're angry." Then, when they're ready, explore whether the grievance justifies abandoning faith entirely.

### 2. Desire for Autonomy

**Signs:**
- "I'm 16, I should be able to decide"
- "You can't force me to believe"
- General pushback against all rules, not just Mass
- Assertion of independence in other areas too

**What's happening:** Normal adolescent development. Teens need to separate from parents and form their own identity. Sometimes faith becomes the battlefield for this separation. They're not rejecting God — they're rejecting parental control.

**Response approach:** Acknowledge their growing independence. "You're right that you need to own your faith. I can't force belief. But I can require respect for our family's practices while you figure things out." Give them space within boundaries.

### 3. Influence from Friends or Romantic Interest

**Signs:**
- New friend or boyfriend/girlfriend who doesn't practice
- Sudden change after starting new relationship
- Repeating phrases or attitudes from someone else
- Spending all their time with new person

**What's happening:** Your teen is prioritizing a relationship over faith. They may be mirroring the other person's views to maintain connection. Or they may be experiencing genuine intellectual influence.

**Response approach:** Don't attack the friend (that backfires). Ask questions about the influence. "How does [friend] think about religion? How has that affected you?" Help them think critically about the influence without forcing rejection of the relationship.

### 4. Moral Compromise (Guilt Keeps Them Away)

**Signs:**
- Sudden refusal after period of secrecy
- Defensiveness when asked about behavior
- "I'm not worthy to go"
- Known moral compromise (sexual activity, substance use, etc.)

**What's happening:** Your teen feels guilty and believes they're unworthy to participate in Mass. Rather than face their guilt, they avoid the situation that triggers it. This is actually a sign of remaining conscience — they still believe Mass matters.

**Response approach:** This requires both truth and mercy. Affirm that they're always welcome at Mass, regardless of their state. "Mass isn't for perfect people. It's for sinners who need grace." Then address the moral issue separately, ideally through Confession.

### 5. Spiritual Dryness

**Signs:**
- "I don't get anything out of it"
- "It's boring"
- "I don't feel anything"
- Long-term gradual decline rather than sudden refusal

**What's happening:** Your teen has never developed personal faith. They went through the motions because they had to, but never internalized the faith. Now that they have choice, they don't see the point.

**Response approach:** This is the hardest cause because there's no crisis to address. You need to help them discover faith as meaningful rather than force attendance as meaningless. Focus on relationship with God rather than religious obligation.

### 6. Laziness (Not Every Refusal Is Deep)

**Signs:**
- Willing to do other fun activities at same time
- "I just don't feel like it"
- No ideological objection, just inconvenience
- Sleeping in, staying up late Saturday night

**What's happening:** Sometimes it's not complicated. Your teen would simply rather sleep, hang out with friends, or do anything else. Mass feels like an obligation competing with leisure.

**Response approach:** Don't over-spiritualize. Treat it like other responsibilities. "I understand you don't feel like it. I don't always feel like going to work. But we do what we're committed to do."

## The Response: What to Do

### Step 1: Don't Force Attendance

**A 17-year-old cannot be physically forced to Mass.** They are too large to carry. Attempting to do so creates resentment and damages relationship. More importantly, forced attendance doesn't achieve the goal — genuine faith.

**What forcing Mass does:**
- Creates resentment toward you and the Church
- Teaches that faith is about external compliance, not internal conviction
- Damages your relationship when you need it most
- Often leads to complete rejection as soon as they leave home

**What to say instead:**
"I can't force you to go to Mass. You're nearly an adult. But I need you to understand why this matters to me, and I need us to have a real conversation about what's going on."

### Step 2: Require Participation in Family Life

While you can't force Mass attendance, you can require respect for family religious practice:

**Boundaries to set:**
- "You don't have to go to Mass, but you do need to join us for family dinner."
- "You don't have to believe, but you do need to be respectful of our faith."
- "While you live here, I expect you not to make plans during Mass time."
- "I expect you to participate in family prayer, even if you're not fully present."

**The principle:** You can't control their internal beliefs, but you can maintain external respect for the family's religious identity.

### Step 3: Have the Conversation

**The opening:** "I notice you've stopped going to Mass. Can you help me understand why?"

**Then: Listen. Really listen.**

Don't argue. Don't lecture. Don't panic. Just seek to understand.

**Questions to ask:**
- "When did you stop wanting to go?"
- "What changed?"
- "Is there something specific that bothered you?"
- "How do you feel about faith right now?"
- "Is there something else going on that I should know about?"

**Listen for:**
- Specific grievances (scandal, hypocrisy, doubt)
- Influences (friends, romantic interests, internet)
- Emotional state (anger, sadness, apathy)
- Practical issues (boredom, scheduling, conflicts)

### Step 4: Respond Based on the Cause

**If anger at Church/scandal:**
"What you're describing is terrible. The Church has failed in serious ways. But does the failure of some Catholics mean Jesus isn't real? Does scandal disprove the Resurrection?"

**If desire for autonomy:**
"I understand you need to make your own decisions. I'm not trying to control your faith. But I do need you to respect our family's practice while you're part of this household."

**If friend/romantic influence:**
"Tell me about [person]. How do they think about faith? How has that affected you?" (Don't attack the person — explore the influence.)

**If moral compromise/guilt:**
"Mass isn't for perfect people. It's for sinners. Some of the greatest saints were great sinners first. You're always welcome, no matter what you've done."

**If spiritual dryness:**
"I hear you saying it feels meaningless. That's hard. Let's talk about what would make faith feel meaningful to you. What are you actually looking for?"

**If laziness:**
"I get that you don't feel like it. But part of growing up is doing what we committed to do, even when we don't feel like it. Mass is a family commitment."

### Step 5: Set Clear Boundaries

**Sample boundary statement:**

"I can't force you to practice your faith. That's between you and God. But while you live in our home, I expect respect for our family's religious practice. That means:

- Not making plans during Mass time
- Not mocking faith or religious practice
- Participating in family prayer
- Not bringing substances or activities into our home that conflict with our values
- Treating our faith with basic respect, even if you don't share it

If you can't respect these boundaries, we need to talk about what living arrangements would work better."

**Note:** The last statement is serious. If they consistently undermine your family's religious practice, they may need to live elsewhere. This is a last resort, but it should be mentioned so they understand the seriousness.

### Step 6: Keep the Door Open

**Always end with hope:**

"I hope you'll come back to Mass. Not because I'm forcing you, but because I believe it's where we meet Jesus. When you're ready — whether that's next week, next year, or ten years from now — I'll be there. No questions asked. No 'I told you so.' Just welcome."

**Then: Pray.**

Pray for their return. Pray for your own patience and wisdom. Pray for the grace to love them unconditionally while maintaining your own faith and boundaries.

## What NOT to Do

### Don't Shame or Guilt-Trip

**Bad:** "After everything I've done for you, this is how you repay me?"

**Why it fails:** Creates resentment, not repentance. Makes faith about obligation to you rather than relationship with God.

### Don't Threaten Eternal Damnation

**Bad:** "You're going to hell if you keep this up."

**Why it fails:** Teen hears manipulation, not truth. Also, you don't know their eternal destiny — that's God's judgment, not yours.

### Don't Compare to Siblings or Other Teens

**Bad:** "Your brother never did this. The Johnsons' kids all go to Mass."

**Why it fails:** Shame and comparison don't change hearts. They just make your teen feel judged and inadequate.

### Don't Pretend Nothing Is Wrong

**Bad:** Saying nothing, ignoring the refusal, hoping it resolves itself.

**Why it fails:** Silence communicates that you don't care or that faith isn't important enough to address.

### Don't Cut Off Relationship

**Bad:** "If you won't go to Mass, don't bother talking to me."

**Why it fails:** Relationship is the only bridge back to faith. Cutting it off destroys the path to return.

## Special Situations

### The Teen Who Was Previously Devout

If your teen was previously engaged in faith and suddenly refuses, something specific happened. Find out what.

- Did they encounter a specific intellectual challenge?
- Did someone hurt them in a church context?
- Did they experience loss or suffering?
- Did they start a new relationship?
- Did they discover something scandalous about Church history or leadership?

Sudden changes usually have specific causes. Identify and address the cause.

### The Teen Who Never Really Believed

If your teen went through the motions but never seemed to internalize faith, their refusal may be less crisis and more honesty. They're finally admitting what was always true.

This requires different approach — more formation, less crisis intervention. They need to encounter faith as meaningful, not just expected.

### The Teen Living at Home vs. Away at College

**At home:** You have more leverage. You can require participation in family religious life. You can observe behavior. You can have daily influence.

**At college:** You have less leverage. You can't force anything. Your influence is relational and conversational. Focus on staying connected and being a resource when they're ready.

---

## The Long Game

### Many Return

Statistics show that many young people who leave the Church in their teens or twenties return later, especially when they marry and have children. Your teen's current refusal may not be permanent.

### Your Faithfulness Matters

Continue going to Mass yourself. Continue praying for them. Continue being a witness to faith's importance in your own life. Your consistency speaks louder than your words.

### Relationship Is the Bridge

Whatever happens with Mass attendance, maintain relationship. A teen who feels loved and accepted is more likely to return to faith than one who feels judged and rejected.

## Prayer for Parents of Teens Who Refuse Mass

"Lord, my child has stopped coming to You in the Eucharist. My heart breaks. I want to force them, to drag them, to make them see what they're missing. But I know I can't force faith.

So I surrender them to You. You are the Good Shepherd who leaves the ninety-nine to find the one lost sheep. Find my child. Call them by name. Draw them back to Your heart.

Give me patience to wait. Give me wisdom to know when to speak and when to be silent. Give me love that doesn't depend on their religious practice. Give me faithfulness to continue seeking You myself, even in my grief.

I trust You with my child's soul. You love them more than I do. Bring them home. Amen."
